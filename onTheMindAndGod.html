REFLECTIONS ON AUGUSTINE

I am currently awed by St. Augustine's Confessions. What a beautiful book. What a beautiful mind. What it takes, to write down all your shortcomings — what beauty, what dignity, what sanctity.

His words got me thinking about the mind. How does the mind know itself? How does it understand itself? It is aware of its own existence — and yet, there seems to be a distance, a chasm between the mind and the knowing of the mind.

Does God have a mind like ours?

We know He has thoughts — or does He? But we know He feels. Anger. Joy. Pleasure. As He said during Jesus' baptism: "This is my son, with whom I am well pleased."

The most common way people explore their minds — is by looking within. But even there, the distance remains.

I wonder: is this part of what Freud tried to study? The mind — that strange thing which produces thought, and lives, somewhere, behind the brain.

But the mind is not essential because it exists. It is essential because of what it produces: thoughts. And thoughts — they shape who we are, even though we barely understand them.

If we cannot understand ourselves, how can we hope to understand You, O God?

God became man, so that we might come closer. He knew man so deeply — He became one. Yet man still does not know Him.

How are we to know You, God? Guide us. Guide us.

We are to read the Scriptures: Proverbs, Ecclesiastes. We are to seek. We are to knock. We are to call:

God. God. God.
